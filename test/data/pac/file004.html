Bottom Line

DANGER LEVEL DEFINITIONS

Summary

The Avalanche hazard today is Considerable on all wind affected slopes. Human caused avalanches are likely on steeper terrain on multiple aspects.  New snow and gusty/variable winds have created new wind slabs on several aspects. These new wind slabs have the potential to be a few inches to 2-3 feet in depth. Additional snowfall over the last 48 hours has added more weight to our snowpack which is still harboring a variety of weak layers on wind protected terrain. The most dangerous of these being a layer of buried surface hoar that has resulted in several good sized avalanches in the last week.

If you are headed out into the mountains today make sure that you and everyone in your group is carrying avalanche rescue gear and knows how to use it. Snowmobiles sidehilling in steep terrain right now are rolling the dice, the current avalanche problem is fairly widespread and an avalanche earlier this week resulted in a very close call for a rider near Brundage Reservoir.

The West Central Mountains received an additional 13+ inches of snow over the last 48 hours.  Accompanying this latest storm we saw variable winds that were easily transporting the light density snow with drifting occurring even in the valley. The last two storms have shared a common theme which is winds that started out coming generally from the SW and eventually shifted all the way around the W side of the compass to N/NW.  These winds have done a great job leaving behind wind slabs on about half of our upper elevation terrain. Variable winds not only load ridgetop locations but they also do a good job cross loading depressions, gullies and other features lower down.  The result is that you may ride or ski into a wind slab below the ridgelines before you even realize that the snow has changed beneath you.  Wind does a good job leaving visual clues and the visibility is going to be pretty good today, take the time to look for sculpted or pillowy looking areas as you are planning your routes through today.  If you are riding and you notice a change in the feel or consistency of the snow beneath you, you are probably on a wind slab and you should look for a quick exit. Some of these wind slabs have the potential to be several feet deep today and you could be unlucky enough to find some E, NE, and N slopes that would be deeper still.

Several persistent weak layers exist within our snowpack right now.  Our winter snowpack is developing a personality and adding depth which means you need to get to know it before you can make decisions about how to treat it.  We have found a fairly widespread layer of buried surface hoar that this week that resulted in several avalanches throughout the last week.  Most notably was one near Brundage Reservoir which buried a rider and his sled.  Buried surface hoar is a dangerous layer and is responsible for several avalanche fatalities each year.  We have found this layer to be healing itself slowly but also found plenty of reasons to be concerned about it.  We have found it in both the lower half of the valley on West Mountain and in the mountains north of McCall. Prior to the latest storm this layer was about 2 feet down, after this storm, it is probably closer to 3 feet in most places. Where this layer is preserved, it has the potential to result in large avalanches that are likely to propagate or spread out once triggered over long distances.  Steep wind protected terrain is exactly where you are most likely to find this layer.   (Crown of avalanche near Brundage Res)

Recent Avalanche Activity:

Nothing new reported in the last few days, we are still hearing 2nd and 3rd hand reports of riders triggering slides Sunday and Monday.  If you are out in the snow and you trigger an avalanche or see new avalanche activity, PLEASE take the time to report it to the PAC.  It helps forecasters paint a better picture of what going on throughout the area.  The information you share may help save a life.  You can click on the Observations button on our site, email us at: forecast@payetteavalanche.org or leave us a message at 634-0400.  Your privacy is always protected, we just want to know what's happening in different parts of our adviosry area.

Current Conditions

Yesterday the snow conditions were one of the best presents anyone could ask for. Deep light and cold, fluffy snow.  Expect more of the same today throughout the backcountry and look for another round of cold storms to enter the area on Saturday.
