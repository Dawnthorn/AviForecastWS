Bottom Line

DANGER LEVEL DEFINITIONS

Summary

The avalanche hazard is Moderate above 6500 feet on all slopes over 35 degrees. Buried weak layers can be found in the snowpack on different aspects and at different elevations. In the upper snowpack we have some wind slabs on most aspects near ridge tops, buried surface hoar, and down around 3 to four feet a layer of facets is holding everything above.

Additional new snow and high winds over the next few days may be the straw that breaks the camels back.

Trend over the next 24 hours

Yesterday in an ENE pit at 8,000 feet we observed 10 inches of new snow sitting on very well preserved surface hoar (weak Layer). We were able to get moderate failures in compression (CT13, and no propigation in an ECTN13 both Q2). In a west pit around 6800 feet we were barley able to make out fragments of surface hoar beneath the new snow, and were able to get similar moderate compression results on this buried surface hoar layer.

 

WINDS---continue to effect North West ,counter clockwise, through North terrain (almost all aspects). We observed cornices growing on East and North East ridges---watch out for slabs on loaded lee slopes. The wind "thickened" the windward slopes that were not protected.

Increasing snow load and strong winds from the South, Soutwest will load North through east terrain significantly. Our current snowpack will likely fail under this new stress. Our earlier shallow snowpack has not healed, and still holds layers that deserve respect/ which is a good reason to travel different, and more cautiously than you have in the past.

Down deeper (around 4 feet) there still remains a weak faceted layer that draws concern, even though it is getting harder to effect this layer, if you do it could be a big one?

Increasing snow load and strong winds from the South, Soutwest will load North through east terrain significantly. Our current snowpack will likely fail under this new stress.

Our early, shallow snowpack is still hiding its scars deep down, which is a good reason to travel different, and more cautiously than you have in the past.

While we have seen scores from stability tests in the last couple of weeks in the moderate range, it does not change the fact that the foundation is much like raw sugar, loose and un-consolidated---exactly what we don't want as a base layer. Added load will likely make this layer fail, or sit and wait for a sled or skier to tip the balance.

It's time to be extra careful, and maybe re-think playing in terrain over 35 degrees.

 

 

Recent Avalanche Activity:

No new avalanches, not even a point release...the snake is coiled, but has not struck.

Other places farther North of us that have recieved more snow, are seeing big avalanches...be careful as much more snow is on the way accompanied by winds upwards of 50MPH this weekend.

Current Conditions

7-10 inches of new snow fell Wednesday night, and two more last night on top of surface hoar. Great skiing and riding is out there on top of some tricky weak layers...Be careful on lee slopes as the wind has effected them as well as almost all other aspects.
