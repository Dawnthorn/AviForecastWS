Bottom Line

DANGER LEVEL DEFINITIONS

Summary

The Avalanche Hazard across the West Central Mountains today is CONSIDERABLE on all slopes approaching 35 degrees with a 12-24 inch soft slab resting on a slick crust.  Heavy snowfall, rising temperatures and strong winds out of the S and SW have created several density changes within the new snow making it possible to trigger all or part of this slab on multiple aspects.

The Hazard on North East and North facing slopes will be HIGH where a combination of heavy snow, wind loading and buried weak layers make it likely we will see natural avalanches today and tomorrow.  Human triggered avalanches are very likely on these slopes. Additionally, winds have been sculpting yesterday’s warmer snow into a stout but touchy hard wind slab on many of the windward aspects. Avoid travelling on these slabs on slopes steep enough to slide today and through the next few days.

Carefully evaluate your terrain choices before committing to them.  Use safe riding and skiing protocol and keep your eyes on your partners.

S, SW and W winds have been sustained in the high teens and 20mph hour range with gusts in the 30 mph range for the last three days. Gusts today are forecasted to get close to 40 mph.  Heavy snowfall and wind loading has allowed leeward slopes to pick up close to 2 feet of new snow over the last few days.  We observed a large slope failure (whumpf) on a NE facing slope that was harboring a layer of Near Surface Facets and Surface Hoar giving this wind loaded slope a one two punch of wind load and a buried weak layer. Extended Column Tests on this layer produced full block failure with sudden collapse at 3 taps from the elbow on this layer buried at 16 inches yesterday. True North slopes are likely harboring some of the same layers. The wind loaded slopes were just barely able to sustain the weight of the new snow load yesterday and are looking for a trigger.  With additional snow and increasing temperatures these slopes will likely start producing natural avalanches today or did overnight. Hard wind slabs were forming on S, SE and W facing aspects feeling the full affect of the wind yesterday as well.  These slabs were cracking under the weight of our snowmobiles and failing when isolated yesterday afternoon.  Many of the newly formed wind slabs are resting on older wind slabs and hard wind crusts on W and NW aspects making it possible to trigger the older deeper slab in addition to the new slab.

We had some great dry, cold snow conditions over the last two days, yesterday the temperatures increased throughout the day and deposited a layer of warmer snow above the cold snow below creating a classic upside down snowpack. This formation created a density change and shallow soft slab that was very easy to trigger on slopes approaching 35 degrees.  The new snow seemed to be bonding moderately well to the crust layer below on all but the steepest slopes.  I would hesitate to jump on any slope today or over the next few days over 35 degrees without carefully evaluating the old snow/new snow interface at the crust or the mid slab weakness created by the rising temperatures.  We also found a layer of preserved and sensitive near surface facets and surface hoar on slopes that did not get direct sun last week. Despite the fact that our snowpack is rounding the corner into spring conditions, the new snow is going to take a little time to heal. Pay attention through the rest of the spring to what happens when temperatures really start going up. With the combinations of crusts and facets lurking in our snowpack, we could see some large late season avalanche activity.

Recent Avalanche Activity:

 

Nothing new reported. Visibility was poor yesterday so we could have passed right next to debris without seeing it.  Expect to see natural activity today and possible tomorrow. Please take the time to email or contact us through Face Book about any activity that you do see or trigger.

Current Conditions

Deep, ski turns were as good as you could want yesterday and sledding was a hoot with nearly bottomless conditions over 6800 feet.   Temperature and wind effect likely will make the skiing a little tougher today but a cooling trend with light accumulations through early next week should improve conditions as we turn the corner into April.
