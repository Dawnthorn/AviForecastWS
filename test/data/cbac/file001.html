Summary

Since yesterday, the winds at higher elevations have remained elevated, steadily blowing in the 10-20mph range with higher gusts, even at lower elevation weather stations.  The passing disturbance did not bring more than a few inches of snow, but did scrub out those valley inversions, with temperatures at all locations sitting in the high teens above treeline, and low 20s elsewhere.  

 

Recent observer reports from around our zone indicate the steady west wind over the last 24 hours has indeed created isolated windslabs above treeline, and in some areas, has been enough to overload the persistent weak layers deeper in the snowpack on northern half of the compass. A large slab on the northwest facing "WSC Bowl" on Mount Baldy, near Paradise Divide yesterday, propagated widely, and is estimated to have run about 1,000 vertical feet.  One of our observers took a great photo of the slide (here).  The northwestern corner of our forecast area (Schofield and Kebler Passes)  received almost a foot of snow last weekend, and in some areas, buried a nasty layer of surface hoar.  We have yet to nail down the distribution of this new weak layer,  and on your outing today in these areas, take the time to dig down and note its presence, or absence.  Elsewhere across our zone, the same investigation should be made, but at the moment, the amount of snow capping this January 11th surface hoar is only a few innocent inches, and less of an immediate problem.  

 

This is the classic Colorado scenario.  Stuck in that mid-January rut, with those orange and yellowy shades in the avalanche report beginning to lose their luster.  Signs of instability continue to trickle into the office, structure and stability tests urge caution and respect.  Have patience. Keep your guard up.  The paranoid usually prevail…especially when those warm, April days roll around and spring corn is abundant and steep lines call our names.

What You Need to Know About These Avalanches


Persistent slabs can be triggered by light loads and weeks after the last storm. You can trigger them remotely and they often propagate across and beyond terrain features that would otherwise confine wind and storm slabs. Give yourself a wide safety buffer to handle the uncertainty.

What You Need to Know About These Avalanches


Loose wet avalanches occur where water is running through the snowpack, and release at or below the trigger point. Avoid terrain traps such as cliffs, gullies, or tree wells. Exit avalanche terrain when you see pinwheels, roller balls, a slushy surface, or during rain-on-snow events.

So when will we see a lower danger in our area?

 

We constantly debate our danger ratings, and argue one way or another if the evidence, gut feeling, or hunch verifies our forecast, or refutes it. Recent avalanches, Snow profiles, explosive testing from nearby operations, field days, weather stations, and submitted observations keep us accurate and well fed with current data.  

 

A few weeks ago, we pegged the danger at moderate, but, with close to a dozen human triggered and remotely triggered avalanches in the span of less than a week, we began to second guess ourselves, and corrected the danger near and below treeline to considerable.  Do we regret our error correction?  Not at all.  Are we hyper aware of keeping a close tab on the state of the snowpack after a misstep like that?  You bet.  

 

The snowpack right now can't be rushed, or made to be something it is not.  That could mean skiing or riding a steep line or big open bowl you've been staring at all season before you have the right conditions, or, that could mean wanting to drop the danger on a public bulletin before data and observations support it.  We still have that persistent slab out there, and as boring as it is for the danger rating to become that endless yellow highway centerline as you cross Nevada, that is what we have.  Explosive work this week near Crested Butte has pulled out several persistent slabs near and below treeline, and snow profiles and instability tests show poor structure, and propagating results.  Observations from trafficked areas still report cracking and collapsing from hard cutting ski turns.  We just aren't there yet for green.
