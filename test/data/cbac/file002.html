Summary

Steady, wet snow fell lightly yesterday and overnight with Schofield reporting 4", CBMR 2" and near town a bit less than 2".  Southeast winds have continued to remain well behaved and have barely gusted above 15mph overnight at 12,000ft.  Temperatures sit in the mid to upper 20s at all elevations at 5am.  

 

Your mother always told you to be thankful for what you're given, and we'll take every flake that falls from the sky these days.  This humble dose of snow should not change avalanche danger much today,  and any storm or windslabs should be isolated and manageable on your backcountry outing. Do note that winds over the last 24 hours have blown from the southeast, a rare wind direction for us, and any shallow windlslabs above treeline may not be quite where you usually find them.  Persistent slab structure remains, and those notoriously steep slopes and rollovers should be not be discounted from producing an avalanche large enough to cause some trouble.  It sounds like a broken record, but it is still midwinter, and its rare to be sitting at low danger this time of year.  Don't let that guard down, and continue to practice good travel habits, and solid communication within your crew.

What You Need to Know About These Avalanches


Persistent slabs can be triggered by light loads and weeks after the last storm. You can trigger them remotely and they often propagate across and beyond terrain features that would otherwise confine wind and storm slabs. Give yourself a wide safety buffer to handle the uncertainty.

Low danger in January?  Is this unusual?

 

 Yes, it is rare, but not totally uncommon.  You probably remember before our big 100 inch storm in the first 10 days of February last year.  Prior to that major avalanche cycle and borderline extreme danger, we saw low danger for a number of days.  Extended high pressure can do that to a relatively shallow snowpack that continental mountain ranges usually see. The faceting process is usually in overdrive metamorphosing those snow grains from strong and cohesive, to weak and sugary, largely due to dramatic daily temperature fluctuations.  We have a great blog post from Zach a while back that dives into this complicated process if you got a little time before your ride to the trailhead picks you up.  Dig in!
