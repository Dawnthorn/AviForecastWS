Wednesday 28 November, 2012.

Date Issued November 28, 2012 at 01:18PM

Valid until Friday 30 November, 2012.

DANGER RATINGS (Make sure you understand the danger level meanings)
OutlookWednesdayThursdayFriday
Alpine MODERATE HIGH HIGH
Treeline LOW CONSIDERABLE CONSIDERABLE
Below Treeline LOW MODERATE MODERATE

 

Confidence: Fair. Field data is limited.

Past Weather: We've seen at most a trace of precipitation in the Island mountains since Monday. Winds have been light gusting to moderate at times and mostly from the south east. Temperatures climbed steadily through the period with freezing levels reaching tree line and warmer air aloft. This is changing at time of writing with the onset of a significant weather system.

Main Concerns: (Avalanche problems)

Wind Slab - Precipitation starting on the east coast at time of writing will bring a total of 70 to 150mm of water by Friday night (depending on location, highest amounts on the west coast). Strong winds will move snow to create wind slabs on west through north to north east aspects and in cross loaded features. Initially these slabs will be limited to the alpine but as freezing levels drop they will become a feature at tree line as well. Avalanches will occur naturally and be easily triggerable by humans and will range in size from small to almost big enough to bury, injure or kill a person.

Persistent Slab - A weak layer of faceted snow crystals at an ice crust has been identified as the cause of some large avalanches in the coast range. We do not have good field information as yet from the high alpine on Vancouver Island but this layer could exist here on all aspects in the alpine. It is possible that a human load could trigger an avalanche up to big enough to bury, injure or kill a person.

Weather Outlook:

A significant frontal system has begun to hit the island at time of writing with rising freezing levels (to fall later), large amounts of precipitation and strong winds.

Wednesday - Precipitation beginning around mid day to bring 20 to 35mm of water. Freezing levels rising to 2000m by later in the day and then falling to around 1700m overnight, Winds strong and mostly from the south east but making their way round to south west as well.

Thursday - 40 to 60mm additional precipitation. Freezing levels dropping toward tree line (snow line a few hundred metres below that). Strong winds continue from the east through south to south west.

Friday - An additional 15 to 35mm of water coming as snow lower down now as freezing levels drop toward about 1200m. Winds continue to be strong.

Avalanche Activity:

No new avalanches have been observed in the past three days.

Snowpack:

The precipitation will come as rain early in the storm thus destroying any surface hoar that may have existed as mentioned in our last report. At tree line and below a stable, progressive density snowpack will get a good soaking before rain turns to snow as our precipitation continues. In the alpine we have yet to confirm the existence in the island snow pack of the crust/facet combination found about 80cm deep in the snowpack at tree line and above in the coast range. Given that the weather conditions which produced this across the pond were much the same on the island, we will continue to assume the presence of this weak layer until proven otherwise. With significant additional load this layer could become a failure point causing large avalanches in the alpine.

Travel Advisory:

Stay out of avalanche terrain in times of  high danger. Stick to lower angled slopes and/or well forested areas. Expect to find wind slab in the lees of ridge crests and in cross loaded features. Be aware that a deep weakness in the snowpack, such as that suspected to exist in our alpine, can produce larger avalanches. Particular areas of concern are alpine start zones with smooth ground cover such as rock slabs, glacial ice or snow from last winter found in shady parts of the terrain and especially where these are adjacent to ares of shallow snow pack.

Prepared by Jan Neuspiel

Monday 26 November, 2012

Date Issued November 26, 2012 at 05:14PM

Valid until Thursday 28 November, 2012.

DANGER RATINGS (Make sure you understand the danger level meanings)
OutlookTuesdayWednesdayThursday
Alpine MODERATE MODERATE HIGH
Treeline LOW LOW CONSIDERABLE
Below Treeline LOW LOW MODERATE

 

Confidence: Fair. Field data remains limited at this time of year.

Past Weather: The Island Alps have seen from zero to 10cm of snow over the past three days. Higher amounts on the west coast, least on the east and about 5cm on the north island. Since our last storm passed winds have been mostly light from the south east through to south west. Freezing levels have been as low as near sea level with a couple of spikes up to near tree line.

Main Concerns: (Avalanche problems)

Persistent Slab - A weak layer of faceted snow crystals at an ice crust has been identified as the cause of some large avalanches in the coast range. We do not have field information as yet from the high alpine on Vancouver Island but this layer could exist here on all aspects in the alpine. It is possible that a human load could trigger an avalanche up to big enough to bury, injure or kill a person.

Wind Slab - Overnight Wednesday and Thursday 30 to 45cm of new snow accompanied by moderate to strong winds mostly from the south east will create wind slab which will fail both naturally and easily under human load. Avalanches could range from small in size to almost big enough to bury a person.

Weather Outlook:

Our ridge of high pressure will start to give way to an approaching warm front.

Tuesday - Mix of sun and cloud, little to no precipitation, winds light gusting to moderate from the south east, freezing level around 1200-1400m with an above freezing layer of air aloft a distinct possibility.

Wednesday: Increasing cloud and light precipitation, winds increasing to moderate and mostly from the south east, freezing levels variable as the warm air overtakes the cold.

Thursday - Heavy precipitation starting later Wednesday with accumulations of 30 to 45mm of water. Winds increasing to moderate to strong south east and freezing levels settling in to somewhere around 1500-1700m (snow level a few hundred metres below that).

Avalanche Activity:

No new avalanches have been observed in the past three days.

Snowpack:

The island snowpack sits at around 130cm to 150cm at tree line. The snow surface now has surface hoar on it which has melted on the sunny aspects but persists in the shade. Storm and wind slab weaknesses in the snowpack reported in our last bulletin have settled out and bonded now. There is a crust around 80cm down into the snowpack which is not of concern at tree line but may well have weak faceted crystals associated with it in the higher alpine. Our forecasters are aiming to get into the alpine this week to investigate this further. If you are making observations of this layer or any observations for that matter please do submit them to us at forecaster@islandavalanchebulletin.com.

Travel Advisory:

Travel is generally safe for Tuesday and Wednesday at and below tree line. Be aware that at the lower elevations hidden obstacles such as stumps and rocks remain a hazard. In the alpine be aware of the possibility of a weak layer deep in the snowpack that could cause larger avalanches especially in areas of smooth ground cover such as rock slabs, glacier ice and patches of last winters snow in the shady parts of the terrain. On Thursday hazard will increase dramatically with the onset of a significant storm. Stay in well forested areas and/or on low angled slopes to avoid being caught in an avalanche on Thursday.

Prepared by Jan Neuspiel

Friday 23 November, 2012.

Date Issued November 23, 2012 at 03:51PM

Valid until Monday 26 November 2012.

Note: We have very limited data in the early season with which to write this bulletin. We will not venture hazard ratings yet but below you will find some information about what is happening in the Island Alps and some general advice for this time of year.

Past Weather: A number of snowfalls and one rain event to the tops of the island alps have left us with a snowpack of around 130 to 180cm at tree line and about 100-130cm at 1000m. Winds have been strong at times making for variable snow depths in the alpine with bare ground in places and much deeper  deposits in others. Our most recent storm overnight Thursday/Friday dropped 20 to 60cm of new snow depending on location and elevation.


Main Concerns: (Avalanche problems)

Wind Slab - Winds during storms have moved snow at tree line and in the alpine to form wind slabs on north east through north to north west aspects and in cross loaded features. These slabs become more difficult to trigger as they b0nd over time but could produce avalanches that while not big enough to bury a person could cause some trouble if combined with a terrain trap like trees or a cliff.

Persistent Slab - Observations in the coast mountains have shown a deeper and persistent weakness in the snow pack in the alpine. Tree line evidence on the island suggests that this weakness may well exist in the island snowpack in the alpine on all aspects. If triggered it could produce avalanches from small to big enough to bury injure or kill a person.

Weather Outlook:

Friday - Precipitation trailing off in the early hours and giving way to a mix of sun and cloud. Freezing level dropping from a high of around 1500m in the early hours of Friday to around 1000m. Winds lightening and from the west and south west.
Saturday - 0 to 5cm of snow. Light to moderate winds from the west. Freezing level 800-1000m.
Sunday - 0 to 5cm of snow. Light to moderate north west winds. Freezing level around 1000m.

Avalanche Activity:

A few natural avalanches to size 1.5 have been observed during and soon after our storms.

Snowpack:

The island snowpack sits at around 130 to 180cm at tree line and about 100-130cm at 1000m. There are density variations within the snow pack due to temperature changes and wind effect during precipitation events. These density changes deliver natural avalanches and easy planar results in tests during and soon after storms but tend to start to bond after that and produce less reactive results. A crust from early November has been observed at tree line. While this crust poses no issues at this elevation, the same crust has been shown to be a persistent weakness in the alpine in the coast range due to faceted snow crystals at the crust. Alpine observations have not been made to confirm this problem exits in the island alps but it would be prudent to assume that it does until more data is collected. Snow distribution in the alpine is highly variable due to strong winds during previous storms with areas bare of snow while others are quite "fat".

Travel Advisory:

At tree line and in the alpine watch for wind slab. This presents as everything from soft slab just slightly more dense snow) to hard slab (little to no ski penetration). Watch for increasing density of snow in wind zones and for cracking and shooting cracks. Test small features for reactivity of slabs. In the alpine be aware that we may have a deeper persistent instability that could cause avalanches large enough to bury, injure or kill a person. Be particularly cautious in areas with smooth ground cover such as rock slabs, glaciers or where last years snow persists beneath. If you have the skills to identify the crust and associated faceted snow crystals mentioned above, do dig around to see if you can find these and to assess how they react in tests. Then send us your results to forecaster@islandavalanchebulletin.com. Any and all observations are of value!
While coverage is pretty good below tree line for this time of year it is still thin enough, especially at lower elevations, that hazards such as rocks, stumps etc still lurk beneath the snow.

Please note: Mount Washington Alpine Resort is not open to the public until Friday 30 November.

Prepared by Jan Neuspiel.

Important Notice

This bulletin covers the mountainous region of Vancouver Island from the Mt. Cain Ski Area in the North to the Beaufort range to the South including the mountains of Strathcona Provincial Park.

This is a regional forecast and significant variation may exist within the forecast area. The information and danger ratings are intended as a trip planning aid for recreational, backcountry users of avalanche terrain; they are not meant to be used as the sole factor in determining the avalanche danger presented by a specific slope.

Always include local weather, snowpack and avalanche observations in your decision to travel in avalanche terrain. Observations and experience may lead to different conclusions from what is reported or recommended. See disclaimer for further details. The technical data used to produce these bulletins is obtained from a variety of sources, including local ski areas and remote weather resources.
