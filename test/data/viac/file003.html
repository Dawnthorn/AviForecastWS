Friday , 6 February 2015

February 6, 2015 at 12:13PM

This Bulletin Valid Until: Sunday, 8 February 6pm 

DANGER RATINGS (Make sure you understand the danger level meanings)

Outlook Friday                 Saturday  Sunday
Alpine CONSIDERABLE HIGH HIGH
Treeline MODERATE CONSIDERABLE CONSIDERABLE
Below Tree line LOW LOW
LOW

Confidence: Poor, Very dynamic weather pattern and fluctuating freezing levels

Main Concerns: (Avalanche problems)

Wet Slab or Wet loose- Rain soaked wet slabs on old crust layer continue to be the main concern in avalanche terrain treeline and above. These could be large in specific areas and respond easily to human triggers.

Wind slab- Freezing levels falling over the weekend and strong southly wind may allow new slab formation at higher elevations in the Alpine on Lees from NE to NW. These avalanches could be large and runout far on old crust. They may occur naturally and be vey touchy to human triggers.

Travel/Terrain Advice: Widespread heavy rain to mountain tops after wednesdays new snow has created dangerous avalanche conditions, continued warm rain and then falling freezing levels will complicate the situation in the alpine requiring careful snowpack evaluation and cautious route finding. Conservative decision making will be essential. Large avalanches could runout long distances on old crust so be aware of terrain above.

Past Weather: Wednesday saw snow accumulating at treeline and above accumulating to 20cm plus above 1600m. Thursday freezing levels rose to 2000m and most areas recieved 80-100mm rain to mountain tops. Some areas of western Strathcona park may have recieved up to 200mm during the same period!

Avalanche Summary :  Thursday Mt Washington Ski Patrol observed and ski cut numerous wet slab and loose wet avalanches at treeline on many aspects. These ran on old crust from 1 Feb and easily entrained (propagated/pulled in surrounding snow) to be size 2( large enough to bury a person) running out to flatter terrain.

Snowpack  Description: All areas have recieved heavy rainfall to the tops of our mountains. Any new snow that fell Wednesday is now very saturated and likely to have released naturally or could with slight human triggers. The mid to lower snowpack below the 1Feb crust is well settled and any lingering instabilities well capped and bridged by the heavy crust. Below treeline the snowpack is becoming very shallow, particularly on solar aspects.

 Weather Forecast:

 Friday- Freezing levels dropping to 1800-1700m, 25-83mm precipitation forecasted, Gale force Southerlies.

Saturday-Freezing level forecast varies from 1100-1600m, 10-43mm precipitation and Strong SW winds.

Sunday-Freezing level 1600m, 7-20mm precipitation and moderate Southerlies.

 Forecaster:Lyle Fast

Wednesday 4 February 2015

February 4, 2015 at 12:30PM

This Bulletin Valid Until: Thursday 5 February 6pm 

DANGER RATINGS (Make sure you understand the danger level meanings)

Outlook Wednesday                 Thursday  
Alpine MODERATE HIGH  
Treeline MODERATE CONSIDERABLE  
Below Tree line LOW LOW
 

Confidence: Good

Main Concerns: (Avalanche problems)

Wednesday

Wind Slab-  SE and SW winds combined with new snow on old crusts have created slab that may release with human triggers, these could be small in specific areas or large in isolated areas.

Thursday

Wet Slab or Wet loose

Rapid warming and rain Thursday will flush much new snow off old crusts treeline and above on steeper terrain. These avalanches could be large in many areas in the Alpine and run naturally or be triggered by humans easily.

 

Travel/Terrain Advice:

 Wednesday

 Avoid areas of wind accumulated new snow that may form slabs on old crust surface, these will be on lee slopes NE thru NW.

Thursday

Thursday would be a good day not to be on avalanche terrain treeline or above as rapid warming and heavy rain will encourage natural wet snow releases on a variety of terrain.

Past Weather: Monday and Tuesday saw up to 14mm precipitation falling as snow above 1200m for some periods. A mix of clear and cloud allowed surface hoar development at times followed by freezing rain. 

Avalanche Summary :  None have been observed or reported.

Snowpack  Description: The crust from 1 February is supportive and bridging the mid and lower snowpack, new snow since varies from nil at 1000m to 15 cm in the Alpine. This new snow from Monday and Tuesday appears to be moderately bonding to the old crust in some observations.

 Weather Forecast:

 Wednesday- Freezing levels rising in afternoon from 1200m-1600m, SE winds rising from light to strong. Precipitation begining late in the day with amounts from 4-8mm.

 Thursday- Freezing levels rising to 1800-2000m, SE winds from moderate to Gale force. Large precipitation amounts from 30-87mm forecasted.

Forecaster:Lyle Fast

Important Notice

This bulletin covers the mountainous region of Vancouver Island from the Mt. Cain Ski Area in the North to the Beaufort range to the South including the mountains of Strathcona Provincial Park.

This is a regional forecast and significant variation may exist within the forecast area. The information and danger ratings are intended as a trip planning aid for recreational, backcountry users of avalanche terrain; they are not meant to be used as the sole factor in determining the avalanche danger presented by a specific slope.

Always include local weather, snowpack and avalanche observations in your decision to travel in avalanche terrain. Observations and experience may lead to different conclusions from what is reported or recommended. See disclaimer for further details. The technical data used to produce these bulletins is obtained from a variety of sources, including local ski areas and remote weather resources.
