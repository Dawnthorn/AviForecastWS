Advisory

Eastern Sierra Avalanche Forecast
Apr 21 2013

Bottom line: 
Today the avalanche danger is estimated to be LOW. Generally safe avalanche conditions exist, although there’s potential for wet snow avalanches on high elevation slopes that get direct sun throughout the day. With daytime heating, sun exposed slopes in alpine terrain will soften today, and there may be isolated areas in steep sun exposed slopes around rocks and cliffs where human triggered wet loose snow slides are possible. Stay off of--and out from underneath--any slope approaching 35 degrees or steeper that is getting wet.
Avalanche Discussion: 
I over forecast the wet activity yesterday. Luckily, the cold morning temperatures and wind kept the snow from warming until the afternoon. An observer riding in the False White area yesterday found supportable snow from 9,400 to 11,000 feet on all aspects. Steep rollovers did not shear off in wet snow slides. It will be warmer today but nights will be cold for this time of year- mid 20’s at the high elevations. North winds will keep the snow surface cool, similar to yesterday. There are a few patches of snow below 8,500 ft- snowline begins around 10,000 ft. High elevation slopes provide the best skiing and riding opportunities. Even though the date is approaching the third week of April, north facing terrain, especially in shaded steep couloirs, retains moist powder and snow temperatures well below freezing. Buried facet layers, leftover from the cold, dry winter, are common in the upper meter of the snowpack. You will find snow transitioning from winter to spring at exposed high elevations. With continued warm temperatures and clear skies, warming will reach the high sheltered alpine areas soon.
Mountain weather: 
Another pleasant day is on tap today. After a cool night, temperatures warm a few degrees to the mid 50’s for the mid elevations and upper 40’s to low 50’s for terrain above 10,000 ft. North winds will increase today, gusting to 30 mph over the ridgetops. As we head into the last couple weeks of April and early May, longer range weather forecasts are suggesting drier than normal through the end of April. Spring runoff has begun, but due to the pathetic snowpack, runoff will be around 50% below historical averages for Mammoth Creek and the Owens River.

This advisory is our best interpretation of snow pack conditions and NWS forecasts issued today. Backcountry travelers should be aware that elevation and geographic distinctions are approximate and that a transition zone exists between upper and lower elevations. Avalanches do not happen by accident and most human involvement is a matter of choice not chance. Most avalanche accidents are caused by slab avalanches that are triggered by the victim of member of the victim's party. Even small slides can be dangerous. Always practice route finding skills and carry avalanche rescue gear. Remember that avalanche danger ratings are only general guidelines. Distinctions between geographic areas, elevations, slope aspects and slope angles should be made. ----- Download the free iPhone app, see http://avalancheforecasts.com.
