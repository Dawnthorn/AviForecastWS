Eastern Sierra avalanche advisory
Posted December 30, 2012 by Sue Burak

The avalanche danger is MODERATE on mid and upper-elevation slopes. Right now more slopes are stable than unstable, but human triggered avalanches remain possible and the avalanche danger is rated MODERATE.Human triggered avalanche are possible on slopes steeper than about 35 degrees in the Mammoth and Rock Creek areas.




 

Light snow is falling this morning in the Mammoth and June Mountain area with morning temperatures in the upper teens. . Rock Creek is picking up light snow and temperatures are in the single digits.

Triggering an avalanche today will be a lot like hitting the bull’s eye on a dartboard. It could take dozens of throws or you could get lucky (or in this case unlucky) on the first toss. People are riding and skiing steep terrain without triggering slides. Right now more slopes are stable than unstable but you still can trigger an avalanche in steep windloaded terrain.

Extended column tests and compression tests done in the Mammoth area at 8,000 ft to 10,000 ft on north aspects over the last two days show a variety of propagating and non-propagating cracks and differences in shear quality. Most of the test results occurred in the upper 12-15 inches of the snowpack. Control work on June Mountain on Friday produced minimal avalanche activity.

The rain layer is deeply buried in the Mammoth Basin and Mammoth Crest. An observer reporting from the 8,000 ft elevation in Mammoth noted the rain crust had turned to facets and ECT’s were not failing on it. It will probably take a huge load to trigger an avalanche on this layer but it still is a deeply buried persistent weak layer.

In the Rock Creek area, the rain crust is reacting in ECT’s and propagation saw tests. This is likely due to a shallower snowpack of about 3 to 4 feet at 9,000 to 10,300 ft.

Today, the primary avalanche problem is wind slabs. Yesterday, gusty southerly winds blow snow onto north aspects along the Mammoth Crest. Southeast winds were light and breezy in the Rock Creek area. Winds today are from the north and upslope snowfall will favor north facing terrain. With more snow falling, expect sensitive soft slabs on leeward slopes. The best approach today will be to recognize and avoid wind loaded slopes, especially those in steep, upper elevation terrain.


The second avalanche concern is the rain crust. Impacting this layer will be easiest on slopes that have a thin and variable snowpack.

Over the past 24 hours 1-2 inches of snow fell in the mountains. Currently, temperatures are in the single digits and north winds are blowing 5-15 mph. Today, temperatures will remain in the upper teens to low 20’s with overnight lows in the single digits. Winds will continue to blow from the north and northeast from 5-15 mph with gusts to 30 mph over high elevation terrain. Skies will be cloudy today as another weak storm system moves east to end the series of storms that began on December 18.


-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- Please note that the avalanche danger rating in this advisory expires in 24 hours. This advisory is our best interpretation of snow pack conditions and NWS forecasts issued today. Backcountry travelers should be aware that elevation and geographic distinctions are approximate and that a transition zone exists between upper and lower elevations. Avalanches do not happen by accident and most human involvement is a matter of choice not chance. Most avalanche accidents are caused by slab avalanches that are triggered by the victim of member of the victim's party. Even small slides can be dangerous. Always practice route finding skills and carry avalanche rescue gear. Remember that avalanche danger ratings are only general guidelines. Distinctions between geographic areas, elevations, slope aspects and slope angles should be made.
