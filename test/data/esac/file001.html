Advisory

Eastern Sierra Avalanche Forecast
Feb 10 2013

Bottom line: 
Cold temperatures and moderate to strong northeast winds continue to form fresh wind slabs in east and southeast facing terrain today. In isolated areas below cross loaded ridges above treeline, there is a pockety MODERATE avalanche danger. Even though slabs will likely be small, they will be sensitive to skier triggering. The avalanche danger will remain LOW on non wind loaded slopes.
Avalanche Discussion: 
Generally safe avalanche conditions exist on most slopes in the Mammoth Basin and Sherwin Ridge area. In all areas, on nearly all mid-elevation slopes without recent deposits of wind-drifted snow you're unlikely to trigger a slab avalanche, because there is not enough new snow to form wind slabs. At the higher elevations there can always be a slope where a weak layer and slab remain unstable, it's wise to maintain safe travel practices - expose one person at a time on or below steep slopes, and always keep your partners in sight. The northeasterly winds picked up yesterday afternoon; they averaged 15-20 mph overnight and are forecast to do the same today. I expect that some shallow, soft slabs have developed near ridgelines, and that these will be sensitive to a person's weight because they're not well bonded to the various old snow surfaces. The mid elevations in Rock Creek picked up 3-5 inches of new snow- wind drifting was minimal on exposed slopes and open areas in the trees from 9,300 ft to 10,000 ft. In Rock Creek, the main concern is any steep slope with a shallow snowpack. It’s easy to find weak faceted snow- it exists everywhere in the Rock Creek drainage at the mid elevations. Alpine terrain in Rock Creek likely has a few inches of new snow on top of hard slabs sitting over facets. Based on a few tests on mid elevation slopes, the added mass of a few inches of dry snow did not create instability. There are no reports from the alpine elevations in Rock Creek.
Mountain weather: 
Morning lows are in the low teens this morning. Daytime highs at the 9,000 to 10,000 ft elevation will reach the mid 20’s today. Strong north and east winds will continue to plague mid and high elevation terrain with 20-30 mph winds at the mid elevations and gusts to 50 mph over the ridgetops. Cold and dry conditions persist until tomorrow when a gradual warmup begins. No snow is in the forecast. Long range outlooks show little change in the position of the high pressure ridge along the West Coast until late in the month.

This advisory is our best interpretation of snow pack conditions and NWS forecasts issued today. Backcountry travelers should be aware that elevation and geographic distinctions are approximate and that a transition zone exists between upper and lower elevations. Avalanches do not happen by accident and most human involvement is a matter of choice not chance. Most avalanche accidents are caused by slab avalanches that are triggered by the victim of member of the victim's party. Even small slides can be dangerous. Always practice route finding skills and carry avalanche rescue gear. Remember that avalanche danger ratings are only general guidelines. Distinctions between geographic areas, elevations, slope aspects and slope angles should be made.
