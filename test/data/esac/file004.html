Eastern Sierra avalanche advisory
Posted January 13, 2013 by Sue Burak

Updated January 13, 2013 - 9:16 am


Near and above treeline, pockets of MODERATE avalanche danger still exist on mostly north to east facing aspects on slopes 35 degrees and steeper due to recently formed wind slabs in the Mammoth Basin. For the Rock Creek area, the avalanche danger is LOW. Please keep in mind that the definition of LOW danger includes small avalanches in isolated areas or extreme terrain.



 

Light to moderate northwest to northeast ridgetops winds will blow again today. Today along the Mammoth Crest there was a smorgasbord of windslabs- thin, soft, a few small hard wind slabs, wind drifts and thicker slabs. The northerly flow of the past few days and abundant dry cold snow created a different pattern of wind loading than what we are used to seeing from westerly winds. Cross loading created surprises in open slopes and shallow north facing gullies. The mid elevations in the Mammoth Basin had thin sun crusts under 4-5 inches of new snow. Crust is found below the recent new snow on N-NE-E aspects where significant warming occurred during Tuesday and Wednesday of last week. Other northerly aspects are free of crust at the old/new snow interface.

Main Avalanche Problem, Mammoth Lakes Basin. Wind slabs.

Plenty of snow has been available for wind transport over the past few days. Wind slab formation has been most prevalent below ridgetops and in open exposed bowls under the Crest. More isolated areas of wind slab will likely continue to form with continued moderate northerly winds. Isolated areas of instability may exist in highly wind affected areas. . In general, areas of instability will be found within greater areas of stable snow. Pay attention to wind loading patterns on the ascent and descent and employ travel practices that minimize risk.

Main Avalanche Problem, Rock Creek. Persistent buried weak layers

A scary persistent weak layer continues to collapse and propagate fractures in ECT and PST tests. The rain that fell in the first part of December created a single crust below about 9,300 ft where there was bare ground. Above 9,300 ft, the rain fell on about 12 inches of snow and there is a crust sandwich with two rotten crusts with large facets filling the sandwich. Sugary loose faceted snow is alos found above the below the crusts.The rain layer has not been found above about 10,800 ft.

As this dry weather drags on, the effect of cold temperatures and clear dry weather on a shallow snowpack is to grow larger facets and weaken the snowpack. A week of clear cold weather changed the dry fresh snow of last weekend to small facets and widespread surface hoar from 10,000 to 11,000 ft. If a significant wet Pacific storm ends the dry spell and deposits a thick slab of new snow on the snowpack in Rock Creek and other areas with buried rain crusts such as the mid elevations above June Lake, dangerous, deep slab avalanches will occur on many aspects.

A cold low pressure system will remain parked over the West with moderate north to northeast flow bringing ridgetop winds in the 15-25 mph range. Temperatures are going to be quite cold for another few days, then very gradually warm to the upper 20’s and low 30’s by mid week.

High pressure aloft continues to deflect storms north and east of the Sierra for cold and dry conditions for the foreseeable future. This is about as cold as it gets.


-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- Please note that the avalanche danger rating in this advisory expires in 24 hours. This advisory is our best interpretation of snow pack conditions and NWS forecasts issued today. Backcountry travelers should be aware that elevation and geographic distinctions are approximate and that a transition zone exists between upper and lower elevations. Avalanches do not happen by accident and most human involvement is a matter of choice not chance. Most avalanche accidents are caused by slab avalanches that are triggered by the victim of member of the victim's party. Even small slides can be dangerous. Always practice route finding skills and carry avalanche rescue gear. Remember that avalanche danger ratings are only general guidelines. Distinctions between geographic areas, elevations, slope aspects and slope angles should be made.
