Advisory

Eastern Sierra Avalanche Forecast
Feb 13 2013

Bottom line: 
The avalanche danger is LOW; human-triggered avalanches are unlikely on most slopes. At upper elevations, it may be possible to trigger thin, fresh wind slabs on slopes 35 degrees or steeper. Maintain safe travel protocols and be alert for isolated wind slabs that have formed beneath ridgetops and along rock ribs in cross-loaded terrain. At middle elevations, new snow from Friday is faceting and sluffing off supportable crusts. Breezy and moderate northeast winds continue to form fresh wind slabs in east and southeast facing terrain today. Despite their shallow depth, they can pose a danger on slopes above terrain traps. A "low" danger rating does not guarantee no avalanches; it's a probability.
Avalanche Discussion: 
The higher elevations warmed up yesterday about 10-15 degrees though it was hard to tell with the northeast winds blowing. Nighttime lows are warming also, signaling the beginning of a warming trend that will last until the weekend. Reports from higher wind exposed terrain in the Mammoth Crest area, show thin wind slabs were sensitive to skier triggering. Slabs are thin- only about 2 inches thick and propagated 10 to 13 feet across in pockets. On the Sherwin Ridge above Mammoth Rock, an extended column test propagated on facets below the most recent sun crust- the overlying slab was a solid 2 ft slab. Everywhere else had small amounts of new snow on top of the crust, then about 12 inches of faceted snow, then stronger snow below. No propagation occurred in a top near the top of the Hose and the entire pack was faceted snow. Farther east in the Bardini Chutes, there was a mostly supportable crust and the snowpack was composed of mostly rounded grains- an unusual occurrence. Observers reported a recent slab avalanche in the main chute with hard debris. Rock Creek is a facet fest at the mid elevations and in shaded glades up to the 10,800 ft elevation. Main Avalanche Problem: WIND SLAB Persistent north winds continue to create small, thin wind slabs along the ridgetops and the upper sections of bowls and gullies. Though small, there is always the possibility that one could find a thicker wind slab that could knock you off your skis. Use normal caution; look at the potential runout to make sure if you did trigger a slide, you won’t slide over a cliff or rock band. Second Avalanche Problem: PERSISTENT SLAB Snow profiles over the last month show a crust/ facet sandwich in the upper parts of the snowpack in the Mammoth area. Rock Creek’s snowpack is entirely composed of faceted snow. This structure exists on non-wind loaded slopes at the higher elevations and in shaded north and east facing aspects at the mid elevations. Occasionally, extended column tests will propagate a fracture, evidence that the facets will come alive if we get a good load. As of January 15th, the Mammoth Pass snow pack had received 22 inches of snow water equivalent (SWE) and had a 100 inch settled base. Yesterday, February 12, it showed 18" SWE and a 96" base. What's it mean? This site is similar to all locations from Rock Creek to June Mountain- it has not really snowed in 3 weeks and has barely snowed since January 11. Mountain snowpacks are supposed to be increasing in depth during January and early February! The lack of snowfall has allowed the snowpack to adjust to its load, but the cold temperatures and periodic days of warm temperatures and clouds have created weak snow sandwiched between crusts in the Mammoth and June Mountain areas. . A "low" danger rating does not guarantee no avalanches; it's a probability. While we are unlikely to trigger slides, we should maintain safe travel practices as insurance in case we find a booby trap.
Mountain weather: 
A few degrees of warming will bring daytime highs to the mid 40’s at the mid elevations and upper 30’s to low 40’s above 10,000 ft. North winds continue, gusting to 25-30 mph. Morning lows are in the low teens. Breezy to moderate north winds will continue through the end of the week. Warming and dry conditions persist until Tuesday of next week when the blocking ridge moves east and possibly sets up over the east coast, allowing storms to move into the West.

This advisory is our best interpretation of snow pack conditions and NWS forecasts issued today. Backcountry travelers should be aware that elevation and geographic distinctions are approximate and that a transition zone exists between upper and lower elevations. Avalanches do not happen by accident and most human involvement is a matter of choice not chance. Most avalanche accidents are caused by slab avalanches that are triggered by the victim of member of the victim's party. Even small slides can be dangerous. Always practice route finding skills and carry avalanche rescue gear. Remember that avalanche danger ratings are only general guidelines. Distinctions between geographic areas, elevations, slope aspects and slope angles should be made. ----- Download the free iPhone app, see http://avalancheforecasts.com.
