Eastern Sierra avalanche advisory
Posted January 9, 2013 by Sue Burak

The danger is generally LOW. For those venturing into the high, steep alpine terrain, pockets of shallow wind slab can be expected in the north to east facing starting zones.

Watch out today for rocky exposed features on east to south facing slopes for wet snow point releases.




 

The wind advisory for high ridgetop winds this afternoon probably means the best skiing conditions in years is going to be blown away.

It has been a sweet 8 days of calm winds and great skiing and riding conditions over the entire forecast area. Yesterdays high temperatures were a balmy 45 at Main Lodge and 52F at the 9300 ft elevation on Mammoth Pass and Rock Creek.

Rock Creek has a shallow snowpack of 3 ft up to about 9,500 ft. Above 9,500 ft, there is about 4 feet of snow with about a 3 foot slab sitting on top of a large facet/rain crust/ large facet sandwich. We saw large depth hoar below the rain layer in the higher elevations of the Rock Creek area recently. The mid elevations in the June Lake area have a similar snowpack structure with about 3 to 4 feet of a cohesive slab on top of the facets and rain crusts. Stability tests continue to show crack propagation across 1 to 7 meter column tests.

A few inches of new snow and strong winds will create wind slabs below ridgetops from the top of mid elevations to the higher terrain at the higher elevations. During the last week, the snow surface weakened and became faceted due to cold clear nights and warm sunny days. Later today and tomorrow, strong winds will form wind slabs on these facets. It is tricky because wind slabs will form on many slopes, but not all slopes have facets under these wind slabs. The new snow will not create enough stress to affect the buried facet/crust layer in the Rock Creek and June Lake area- that scenario will occur when the dry spell ends.

The main avalanche problem today and for the next few days is wind slabs. There is a plentiful source of dry snow available for the wind to form slabs. Watch out for steep north to east facing slopes along the Mammoth Crest and north to east facing sloeps in the Rock Creek and June Lake areas.

The second avalanche problem is any steep slope with a shallow snowpack.Upper elevation, rocky terrain will be the most likely areas to find weak, faceted snow.The snowpack in the Rock Creek and mid elevations of the June Lake area is shallow and weak, but not unstable. If you are able to push your ski pole to the ground, finding another slope to ride will likely be in your best interest.

The weather is about to change from mild January temperatures and breezy ridgetop conditions to very cold and windy January weather.

Ahead of the cold front, windy conditions are expected today with gusts 40-55 mph expected in the mid and higher elevations. The cold front will move across the Sierra by Thursday morning, producing a short period of snow which may produce an inch of snow. Much colder temperatures are expected Thursday with highs only in the lower 20’s and teens on Thursday. Lows Thursday night will range from the single digits with temperatures dropping below zero. Temperatures will remain 20 degrees below normal through the upcoming weekend.


-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- Please note that the avalanche danger rating in this advisory expires in 24 hours. This advisory is our best interpretation of snow pack conditions and NWS forecasts issued today. Backcountry travelers should be aware that elevation and geographic distinctions are approximate and that a transition zone exists between upper and lower elevations. Avalanches do not happen by accident and most human involvement is a matter of choice not chance. Most avalanche accidents are caused by slab avalanches that are triggered by the victim of member of the victim's party. Even small slides can be dangerous. Always practice route finding skills and carry avalanche rescue gear. Remember that avalanche danger ratings are only general guidelines. Distinctions between geographic areas, elevations, slope aspects and slope angles should be made.
