That is a lot of weather info. What does it mean for us? The temperatures in the start zones have held below freezing so far, with all precip falling as snow. This might change later this morning … or it might not. Overall there is a fair bit of moisture coming in for us with this storm.

The snowpack is beginning to build in our start zones and this allows for slabs to become more uniform across the slope. The primary avalanche problem is going to be new storm snow forming on top of recent surface hoar and a fairly constant ice crust.

While there is the potential for new small slabs to entrain some of the cold dry loose snow underneath the crust sizes are still not likely to impact structures or reach Thane Rd.

So basically there is a strong likelihood of natural avalanches but the sizes are not going to be large enough to reach most developed areas.

The exception to that is the Flume and Perseverance trail systems, where hazard will be high throughout this storm cycle.

Danger for hikers and backcountry travelers will be increasing through the day with new slabs likely to fail with human triggers. Remember that a slide doesn’t have to be big enough to hit a house to cause problems for a skier/sledder/hilker!

Today's Avalanche Tip

WIND

The wind is known as the architect of snow, because it causes the formation of snow slabs and snow drifts as well as many other features out of freshly fallen snow or even older snow that has been there for days. Thereby snow particles are crushed, conveyed and deposited as drift snow.

Against the wind the hoar frost grows as also the tips of the sastrugi. Snow cornices in contrast grow in the same direction, as the wind similar to the cometary tail, which looms behind obstacles. Dunes and wind craters are clear signs of wind activity.

The direction of surface winds is dependent on the prevailing wind direction of the respective weather conditions, the wind force and the terrain relief. Drift snow is found not only in lee areas close to the ridge from windward to leeward, but also in gullies and shallow basins by the wind blowing parallel to the slope.

This cross-loading is the most typical type of loading in our urban start zones.
