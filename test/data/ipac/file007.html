Avalanche Advisory, 12/13/201312/13/2013

Good morning this is Eric Morgan with the avalanche advisory for today, December 20th, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight December 20th, 2013. 

Mountain Weather 

A stronger winter storm will pass through the forecast area today likely to deliver the heaviest snows of the season through tonight.
Today:  100% chance of snow with 3-5 inches, 16 degrees, SW wind 3-5 mph
Tonight:  60% chance of less than 1 inch of snow, 16 degrees, W wind light.
Outlook
Saturday: mostly cloudy with a high of 23, 10% chance of snow later in the day.
Sunday:  30% chance of less than one inch snow, 23 degrees.
The avalanche danger can be expected to increase with the forecast weather for the outlook period.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
With a snowpack well below normal it can be a challenge to reach the high country which we found out yesterday with some other snow machine groups in search of a path to the high country near Jeru peak.  The brush has yet to be covered.  The conditions have not changed a whole lot since last week, consisting of 3-4 feet in the upper elevations with crusts from 11/15 and 12/1 still in there.  The terrain was pretty bony, with some rotten snow in the lower pack with some crust layers throughout.  All of the observations that have been coming in throughout the panhandle have been similar....shallow pack, exposed hazards, and variable conditions.  The good news is that the surface conditions are decent with skier penetration about 3 inches.  The weak layers within the pack are still persisting on all aspects with facets around the crust layers.  Once we get some sort of slab on top of the current pack such as today's predicted snow and any windloading, we must keep our heads on a swivel, searching and being aware of where the slabs form and the increased depths in wind deposited areas. The avalanche hazard rating for the Selkirk and Cabinet Mountains is MODERATE on  slopes steeper than 35 degrees above 5,000 feet.  On slopes less than 35 degrees or below 5,000 feet the hazard is rated as LOW. 

As we all know, avalanche conditions are dynamic and can change for better or worse continually. Use this forecast as a tool in your box.  Backcountry travelers should be prepared to assess current conditions for themselves, plan their routes of travel accordingly, and never travel alone. Backcountry travelers can reduce their exposure to avalanche hazards by utilizing timbered trails and ridge routes and by avoiding open and exposed terrain with slope angles of 30 degrees or more. Backcountry travelers should carry the necessary avalanche rescue equipment such as a shovel, avalanche probe or probe ski poles, a rescue beacon and a well-equipped first aid kit.

Good morning this is Kevin Davis with the avalanche advisory for today, December 13th, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight December 13th, 2013. This is the first weekly avalanche advisory of the season.  We have been getting observations from the mountains from many of you.  Take a look at what people are seeing from across the forecast region on Avy Obs. 

Mountain Weather 

Some weak storms will pass through the forecast area today and for the outlook period.
Today:  30% chance of 1/2 inch of snow, 30 degrees, and SW winds at 6-8mph.
Tonight:  80% chance of less than 1 inch of snow, 23 degrees, W wind a 6mph.
Outlook
Saturday: 80% chance of 1-2 inches of snow, 29 degrees, SW winds at 7-9mph.
Sunday:  40% chance of less than one inch snow, 31 degrees, SW winds at 9-15mph.
The avalanche danger will stay the same with the forecast weather for the outlook period.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
Going up Trestle Creek yesterday we stayed mainly on the traveled routes on the sleds.  The pack is relatively thin and there are alot of trees, shrubs, and rocks poking up through the snow.  On skis we headed up toward Trestle Peak and dug pits on west and east aspects.  In both pits we found about 4.5 feet of snow at 6,000 feet elevation.  Weak layers on both aspects were near the surface in the new snow and deeper in the pack with faceted snow under the crust layers.  Nothing too hazardous at the moment since we are lacking a significant load on these weak layers.  These next storms won't add much to the load but pay attention to the upper layers over the uppermost crust.  Also be aware of heavier loading in wind deposition areas.  We did get an isolated windslab to move on top of the crust with a cornice break.  The avalanche hazard rating for the Selkirk and Cabinet Mountains is MODERATE on  slopes steeper than 35 degrees above 5,000 feet.  On slopes less than 35 degrees or below 5,000 feet the hazard is rated as LOW.

This is Kevin Davis from the Idaho Panhandle National Forest Avalanche Center. Today is October 10th, 2013. This is a preseason update to let you know that we are still here and yes, we did notice that snow in the mountains.  IPAC is looking forward to another winter of avalanche forecasting and education.  We have plans to cooperate more with The Friends of IPAC to make our advisory better and offer more avalanche classes and workshops to the public.  If you have a group that would benefit from taking one of our classes let us know.  We offer a variety of classes to Search and Rescue, law enforcement, ski patrol, and winter sports clubs.  If we can't help you out we're connected with others who work in the snow sports and education field.  We'll be making improvements to our website so keep us on your favorites list and if you have suggestions contact us.  If you are interested in getting involved with IPAC you can assist through The Friends of IPAC and help with avalanche awareness classes for local schools, planning events, and getting out with experienced forecasters for the avalanche advisory.
Looking at the possibilities for Winter 2014, check out this website: http://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/products/predictions/90day/.  By the looks of things there is a fair chance that we will have early snows in the mountains accompanied by milder temperatures.  The longer range looks like an equal chance for precipitation and temperatures to be above or below average conditions.  This seems to be backed up by ENSO (El Nino Southern Oscilliation) forecasts of neutral through this winter.  You might also want to see what Hayden Lake's Cliff Harris says, or my go to, Bill Neimi from Sandpoint.  I personally am going with the fact that this was an exceptional year for bees, they're still hanging on in places, and that gives us an above average chance for heavy snowfall this winter.  Speaking of hanging on, there may be high elevation north faces that could hold this early season snow.  Watch for more early season snow that may accumulate and stick on sheltered slopes.  If it gets to be a foot deep or more and then sits around for a few weeks and recrystallizes, that is the forming of a rotten base and could become a dangerous early season weak layer.  Hunters, pay attention to steep terrain with more than a few inches of snow, especially wind-blown snow, it could slide.  It's time to start paying attention, winter is coming.  I'm 100% certain of that.

Good morning this is Kevin Davis with the avalanche advisory for today, March 29th, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight March 29th, 2013. Special thanks to Idaho Parks and Recreation for sponsoring this morning's advisory.  Thanks to everyone who assisted IPAC with the advisory and classes this winter, we really appreciate your help.  This will be the last advisory for this winter.  We'll post the spring riding tips next week.  Spring is here!

Mountain Weather 

Mild weather will persist over North Idaho for the weekend and we may not get overnight freezing.
Today:  Chance of showers, 5,900' freezing level, 41 degrees, SW wind at 6-9mph.
Tonight:  Partly cloudy, 31 degrees, and SW winds 7mph.
Saturday: Mostly sunny, 45 degrees, and SW winds 3-7mph.
Sunday:  Mostly sunny, 48 degrees, light winds.
The avalanche danger will depend on solar intensity, aspect, and whether it freezes at night.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
It was warm in the mountains yesterday.  The surface snow was wet and heavy and that was at 6,000 feet.  Over a crust you'll find about 1 foot of wet snow on E, N, and W aspects.  South aspects are mostly firm where sheltered and melted crust where exposed.  The layering of the upper pack does pose some concerns for when the sun becomes intense or the thermometer goes over 50 degrees.  With the isothermal, wet snow over the crust the heating from the sun will concentrate above that crust and create the possibility for triggering wet slides on the crust.  I got some of it moving yesterday on a steep west aspect and it was like flowing concrete.  Hard to get out of too.  These conditions can improve but we need freezing temperatures overnight and then you have to plan your route to get off slopes as they are heating up.  Not much of a freeze last night so expect that today you'll want to watch stability of that wet slab over the crust.  It snowed a little late in the day so you may find dust on crust if the surface froze enough.The avalanche hazard rating for the Selkirk and Cabinet Mountains is MODERATE on  slopes steeper than 35 degrees above 5,000 feet.  On slopes less than 35 degrees or below 5,000 feet the hazard is rated as LOW.

Good morning this is Kevin Davis with the avalanche advisory for today, March 22nd, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight March 22nd, 2013. Special thanks to Idaho Parks and Recreation for sponsoring this morning's advisory.  Thanks also to Tom Eddy with Schweitzer Mountain Ski Patrol for observations in the storm this week.  

Mountain Weather 

March powder will persist today with cool temperatures light winds and a chance of more snow.
Today:  70% chance of 2-4 inches of new snow, 25 degrees, W wind at 3-8mph.
Tonight:  40% chance of light snow, 16 degrees, and NW winds 5-9mph.
Saturday:  40% chance of less than half inch of new snow, 26 degrees, and calm winds.
Sunday:  Mostly sunny, 29 degrees, with NE winds at 5mph.
The avalanche danger will remain for today but will decrease with the expected weather this weekend.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
While out in the Selkirks yesterday we found great snow.  We had about 2 inches of new in the last 24 hours and 7 inches of new light snow in the last 48 hours.  Surface snow was a little firm on exposed west aspects but in the trees it was deep and light.  Exposed south aspects are mostly scoured to the sun crust and firm.  East and north aspects are the ones you'll want to be careful on since they are heavily windloaded from strong westerly winds on multiple days this week.  A weak layer of faceted snow is buried about 1.5-2 feet deep just below the ridgelines on N and E aspects and the slab is just firm enough to propagate to a larger avalanche.  Give this layer another day or two to settle under the new snow and even then, watch the big steep terrain on windloaded aspects.  In places you'll also find very large graupel that forms a weak layer about 3-4 inches deep.    The avalanche hazard rating for the Selkirk and Cabinet Mountains is CONSIDERABLE on windloaded slopes steeper than 35 degrees above 5,000 feet.  Below 5,000 feet and on slopes less than 35 degrees the hazard is rated as LOW.

Good morning this is Kevin Davis with the avalanche advisory for today, March 15th, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight March 15th, 2013. Special thanks to Idaho Parks and Recreation for sponsoring this morning's advisory.  

Mountain Weather 

Mild weather will persist today and into Saturday and then another storm system moves in from the coast with colder temperatures and snow.
Today:  Partly sunny, 38 degrees, SW wind 11-16mph with stronger gusts.
Tonight:  Mostly cloudy, 30 degrees, and W winds 6-11mph.
Saturday:  60% chance of less than 1 inch of new snow, 33 degrees, SW winds 6-16mph.  80% chance of 2-4 inches of snow overnight with SW winds increasing to 17-20mph.
Sunday:  70% chance of 2-4 inches of new snow, 29 degrees, and W winds 11-15 with higher gusts.

The avalanche danger will decrease by Saturday with colder temperatures but watch for windloading of new snow over a firm crust.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
Let me first say that the warmer weather has been nice and we have not reached below freezing for a couple of days now, not even at night.  4AM temperatures in the mountains are still in the mid to high 30s, but dropping slightly.  Combine that with the rain in the pack and you'll notice we have very wet conditions.  Because of a melt-freeze crust the rain only percolated down about 4 inches where we were north of Schweitzer.  On a steep east face we got a couple of small slush slides to trigger at the slush layer and this got us thinking that steep, exposed terrain could be hazardous.  Some surface hoar layers are still identifiable at 4 inches and almost 2 feet deep.  A couple crust layers are providing strength above the surface hoar layers but with the warmer weather be aware that the crusts may not be solid.  Make wise terrain choices while the snowpack is warm.  It will tighten up by tonight, we'll have another crust, and possibly another avalanche concern with the new snow on that crust.  The avalanche hazard rating for the Selkirk and Cabinet Mountains is MODERATE on slopes steeper than 35 degrees with pockets of CONSIDERABLE on windloaded north aspects above 5,000 feet.  Below 5,000 feet and on slopes less than 35 degrees the hazard is rated as LOW.

Good morning this is Eric Morgan with the avalanche advisory for today, March 8th, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight March 8th, 2013. Special thanks to Idaho Parks and Recreation for sponsoring this morning's advisory.  

Mountain Weather 

Dry and stable weather with sunshine will persist through the weekend in the Idaho Panhandle with wetter weather next week.
Today:  Partly sunny, 29 degrees, light and variable wind with temperatures dropping to 22 at night.
Tomorrow:  Mostly sunny, 30 degrees, Northwest wind 5 mph, temperatures down to 22 at night.  .
Sunday:  cloudy with a 20 percent chance of snow, high temperatures 33, winds SW8-13.

 Avalanche danger predicted to be CONSIDERABLE today becoming more unstable with warming temperatures on solar aspects.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
The good news is that we got some fresh snow!  The not so good news is that the new 10 inches of fresh (twice as much on windloaded aspects) is sitting on top of an ice crust layer from last weekend’s rain event that has some small mixed surface hoar from Mondays sunshine and some faceted snow that came in as a trace with the cooler weather.  We saw a very reactive upper snowpack in the Caribou drainage of the Selkirks that was sliding on this ice crust on all aspects, something that we have not seen this year and do not see that often.  Everywhere we went, fracture lines were shooting out throughout our travels.  Exciting to see, but use caution.  Expect to see Windloaded aspects propogating big time where the new snow is deeper and more dense.  As the temperatures warm this weekend and solar radiation hits the slopes, be aware.  It may be wise to let this new stuff settle out a day or two on steeper slopes to build more confidence in the pack with further evaluation.   The avalanche hazard rating for the Selkirk and Cabinet Mountains is rated as CONSIDERABLE on slopes steeper than 35 degrees above 5,000 feet on all aspects.  Avalanche hazard is rated as Moderate on slopes less than 35 degrees.  The avalanche danger is expected to increase with warming temperatures before it begins to stabilize, bond and consolidate.

Avalanche conditions change for better or worse continually. Enjoy the sun, but be cautious and have a safe and pleasant weekend!

Avalanche Advisory, March 1st, 2013

03/01/2013

0 Comments

 
Good morning this is Kevin Davis with the avalanche advisory for today, March 1st, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight March 1st, 2013. Special thanks to Idaho Parks and Recreation for sponsoring this morning's advisory.  Thanks also to Schweitzer Mountain Ski Patrol for observations from the mountain and Lance Gidley for assisting with the avalanche hunting yesterday.  

Weather 
Today, the NWS is calling for an 80% chance of precipitation, note - not snow, and the accumulations will be nil.  Freezing level should be around 5,500 feet but look for variations on this.  At  6AM temperatures around the region were at 34 degrees at about 5,000 feet.  High temperature today will be 38 and winds will be out of the SW at 15mph with gusts to 22.  Tonight, will be mostly cloudy, highs in the low-30s and SW winds 11-14mph.  The outlook calls for a 60% chance of rain to 5,800 feet, 40 degrees, and SW winds 8-10mph on Saturday.  By the evening temperatures drop and it will begin to snow, with 3-5 inches expected.  Look for SW winds at 9-14 transporting snow.  Sunday it keeps snowing with an additional 2-4 inches and increasing west winds.  Avalanche danger could peak at CONSIDERABLE today or this evening with warming temperatures and rain and decrease with colder temps by Saturday evening.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
No solid overnight freeze last night is the first thing to consider.  Up on Trestle Ridge in the Cabinets we found fairly stable snow on all aspects but a couple layers, especially on a W aspect had us staying off the steep and exposed slopes.  What you'll find in the pack on most aspects is stabilizing weak layers in the new snow from this week about 8 inches deep and some graupel layers, BB's, one at about 16 inches and the other deeper, about 2 feet.  The upper 2-3 feet of pack has changes in density due to storm snow changes and older sugary layers that have faceted.  There is an old surface hoar layer that has stabilized much but still shears with moderate force at 1.5 feet deep.  Eric was up on Abandon Peak in the kirks and found good conditions, the buried surface hoar layer 2 feet deep, and some shallower weak layers around decomposing crusts.  Warming temperatures may create some problems with the pack depending on how fast it warms and if we get rain.  It doesn't seem like we'll get that much rain and that could save us from potentially high hazard.  I'd just play it safe today and tomorrow during the thaw and wait for the good stuff come Sunday.  If we're lucky the surface won't refreeze into a brick.  The avalanche hazard rating for the Selkirk and Cabinet Mountains is rated as MODERATE  on slopes steeper than 35 degrees above 5,000 feet but could increase to Considerable this afternoon with rapid warming and possibly rain.  Avalanche hazard is rated as Low on slopes less than 35 degrees.  

As we should all be aware....Avalanche conditions change for better or worse continually. Backcountry travelers can reduce their exposure to avalanche hazards by utilizing timbered trails and ridge routes and by avoiding open and exposed terrain with slope angles of 30 degrees or more. Backcountry travelers should carry the necessary avalanche rescue equipment such as a shovel, avalanche probe or probe ski poles, a rescue beacon and a well-equipped first aid kit.

Have a safe and pleasant weekend.

Good morning this is Eric Morgan with the avalanche advisory for today, February 22, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight February 22, 2013. Special thanks to Idaho Parks and Recreation for sponsoring this morning's advisory.  Thanks also to Schweitzer Mountain Ski Patrol for observations from the mountain and Scott Rulander for assisting with the avalanche hunting yesterday.  

Weather 
Today, NWS is calling for 4-8 inches of new snow!!! 26 degrees, and W winds at 10-15 increasing to 24 with 37mph gusts .  Tonight,  will be mostly snowy with temperatures dropping to the upper teens and SW winds up to 30mph, picking up to 4 more inches, possibly more in the Cabinets.  This seems to be bringing in a warm moist system for Saturday.  The outlook for the weekend calls for 70% chance of a few more inches of new snow Saturday with high temperatures around 26 degrees and continued W'erly winds in the 20mph range.  Saturday night snowfall tapers to 1 inch possible, temperatures down to 20 with winds subsiding. Sunday weather continues to be mostly cloudy and cool, less winds and maybe another 1/2 inch of snow.  Avalanche danger will increase due to new snow and windloading for the outlook period.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
Yesterday Scott and I headed into the backcountry out of Schweitzer expecting to see low hazard conditions, stuck our head in the snow, once on accident, and what did we find....but a couple layers of Surface Hoar!!! about a foot and a half deep, failed with an Extended Column Test ECP18 Q1 and Compression Test CTM16 Q1, moderate force, but shears were fast and clean.  There was another surface hoar layer about 4 inches down that did not react....Yet!!!!  I am concerned with the new snow and the windloading taking place and the stability of those surface hoar layers today and Saturday. There is alot of variability in the snowpack and the distribution of the surface hoar, but I found it on a NW aspect and I am sure it is to be found elsewhere where slopes are sheltered from the wind and sun.  We had a Rutschblock Test RB-4 (one jump) on the 18 inch deep surface hoar layer with lots of energy on a 40 degree slope. New snow will be loading onto the weak layers identified and will require the snowgoer to be on their game.  The avalanche hazard rating for the Selkirk and Cabinet Mountains is rated as Considerable  on slopes steeper than 35 degrees above 5,000 feet.  Avalanche hazard is rated as Moderate on slopes less than 35 degrees.
