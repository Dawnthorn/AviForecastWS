Avalanche Advisory, March 15, 2013

03/15/2013

0 Comments

 
Good morning this is Kevin Davis with the avalanche advisory for today, March 15th, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight March 15th, 2013. Special thanks to Idaho Parks and Recreation for sponsoring this morning's advisory.   Thanks also to Ty Foltz with Silver Mountain Ski Patrol for observations from the mountain, and Kyle for the cool glide crack pictures in the St. Regis Basin.

Mountain Weather 

A slight chance of trace amounts of snow today and then another storm system moves in from the coast On Saturday with colder temperatures and snow.
Today:  60% chance of 1/2 inch or less, 42 degrees, SW wind 10-13mph with stronger gusts.
Tonight:  60% chance of 1/2 inch or less, 31 degrees, and SW winds 7-10mph.
Saturday:  70% chance of 1-2 inches of new snow, 35 degrees, SW winds 6-10mph.  100% chance of 2-4 inches of snow overnight with SW winds increasing to 13-17mph.
Sunday:  90% chance of 2-4 inches of new snow, 28 degrees, and W winds 21-23 with higher gusts.

The avalanche danger will decrease by Saturday with colder temperatures but watch for windloading of new snow over a firm crust.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
50 degrees at 5,000 feet in March is a great thing, but only when its followed by more snow, which is what we're supposed to get.  4AM temperatures in the mountains are still in the mid to high 30s, but dropping slightly.  Carole was up at Bullion Pass with Steve Karkenen yesterday and saw some similar surface instabilities that we saw in the Selkirks.  Snow above a crust that became saturated with rain and that was producing moderate shears about 4 inches deep.  Look for this to be a concern in steeper terrain where you could get caught up in a wet slide that may not be that big but it could take you for a ride.  There is potential for slides like this to trigger deeper weak layers in the pack.  Crust layers may not be as strong and supportive while the weather is warm so make wise terrain decisions.  On Silver, Ty had some easy shears about 1 foot deep on a crust on a north aspect earlier in the week. It will tighten up by tonight, we'll have another crust, and possibly another avalanche concern with the new snow on that crust.  The avalanche hazard rating for the St. Regis Basin and Silver Valley is MODERATE on slopes steeper than 35 degrees above 5,000 feet.  Below 5,000 feet and on slopes less than 35 degrees the hazard is rated as LOW. 

Avalanche conditions change for better or worse continually. Use this forecast as a tool in your box.  Backcountry travelers should be prepared to assess current conditions for themselves, plan their routes of travel accordingly, and never travel alone. Backcountry travelers can reduce their exposure to avalanche hazards by utilizing timbered trails and ridge routes and by avoiding open and exposed terrain with slope angles of 30 degrees or more. Backcountry travelers should carry the necessary avalanche rescue equipment such as a shovel, avalanche probe or probe ski poles, a rescue beacon and a well-equipped first aid kit.

Avalanche advisory, March 8th, 2013

03/08/2013

0 Comments

 
Good morning this is Eric Morgan with the avalanche advisory for today, March 8th, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight March 8th, 2013. Special thanks to Idaho Parks and Recreation for sponsoring this morning's advisory.   Thanks to Silver Mountain Ski Patrol for Observations this week.

Mountain Weather 

Dry and stable weather with sunshine will persist through the weekend in the Idaho Panhandle with wetter weather next week.
Today:  Partly sunny, 34 degrees, Northeast winds 5-8 becoming calm with temperatures dropping to 23 at night.
Tomorrow:  Mostly sunny, 34 degrees down to 24 at night.  West wind 3-6 mph.
Sunday:  Mostly cloudy with a high temperature of 38.

 Avalanche danger predicted to be CONSIDERABLE today possibly becoming more unstable with warming temperatures on solar aspects.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
The good news is that we got some fresh snow with sunshine on the way!  The not so good news is that the new 10 inches of fresh is sitting on top of an ice crust layer from last weekend’s rain event.  Yesterday Dan and Carole were near Lookout pass with findings of wind transported snow on the slopes all around, and in the upper Copper, Hanaker, and St. Regis Basin  there were some good cornice formations near the divide.  The problematic layer they observed was just at about 3 feet deep where a persistent weak layer of surface hoar exists.  Ty Foltz reported big slides resulting from explosive control at Silver on Thursday.  He even noted a natural slide on a north aspect that ran on last weekends crust layer.  Temperatures are forecasted to be a bit warmer down there which will increase the amount of stress that the new snow will add on the surface hoar layer.  Watch for more reactivity on the ice crust and the windloaded steeper slopes with the warming where the snow is more dense and becomes more dense.   As the temperatures warm this weekend and solar radiation hits the slopes, be aware.  It may be wise to let this new stuff settle out a day or two on the steeps, let the natural avalanches have their course. The avalanche hazard rating for the Silver Valley and St. Regis Basin  is rated as CONSIDERABLE on slopes steeper than 35 degrees above 4,500 feet on all aspects.  Avalanche hazard is rated as Moderate on slopes less than 35 degrees.  The avalanche danger is expected to increase with warming temperatures before it begins to stabilize, bond and consolidate.

Avalanche conditions change for better or worse continually. Use this forecast as one and not your only tool in your box.  Backcountry travelers should be prepared to assess current conditions for themselves, plan their routes of travel accordingly, and never travel alone. Backcountry travelers can reduce their exposure to avalanche hazards by utilizing timbered trails and ridge routes and by avoiding open and exposed terrain with slope angles of 30 degrees or more. Backcountry travelers should carry the necessary avalanche rescue equipment such as a shovel, avalanche probe or probe ski poles, a rescue beacon and a well-equipped first aid kit.  A knowledgeable and dependable partner is always a plus as well.

Enjoy the sun, but be cautious and have a safe and pleasant weekend!  Remember “is the slope steep enough to slide today”

Avalanche Advisory, March 1st, 2013

03/01/2013

0 Comments

 
Good morning this is Kevin Davis with the avalanche advisory for today, March 1st, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight March 1st, 2013. Special thanks to Idaho Parks and Recreation for sponsoring this morning's advisory.  Thanks to Ty Foltz at Silver Mountain for the obs, and Joel Kopf down south out of the Clearwaters.

Weather 
Today, the NWS is calling for an 80% chance of less than 1 inch of new snow above 5,500 feet, with temperatures rising to 38 degrees and SW winds at 15-18 and gusts to 25mph.  At  6AM temperatures around the region were at 34 degrees at about 5,000 feet.  Tonight will be cloudy and temperatures only dropping to 34 degrees with S winds at 15.  The outlook calls for cloudy on Saturday with temperatures up to 39 degrees and SW winds at 5-9.  By the evening though you can expect a 100% chance of 3-7 inches of new snow falling at 28 degrees with SW winds at 10-14 and gusts to 18mph.  Sunday shows 2-4 more inches of snow, 30 degrees, and W winds.  Avalanche danger may peak today with warming temperatures and possibly rain and then decrease by Saturday evening with colder weather.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
In the Basin yesterday, Carole and Dan fond some weak layers in the upper storm snow around changes in density.  These should be stabilizing as they settle.  Watch for movement in these surface weak layers as the temperatures warm today.  They found some degree of shearing in these layers on multiple aspects.  Deeper in the pack they got moderate shears on the buried surface hoar layer almost 3 feet deep.  It would take alot to trigger this but with conditions rapidly changing today, and the possibility of rain, it would be best to play it safe and avoid steep, exposed terrain, especially where wind-loaded.  On Grandmother Mt at 6,000 feet, Joel found a more stable pack with some moderate shears in light new snow about one foot deep.  The avalanche hazard rating for the Coeur d Alene and St. Joe Mountains is rated as MODERATE on slopes steeper than 35 degrees above 5,000 feet and could rise to Considerable this afternoon with rapidly rising temperatures and possibly rain, especially on wind-loaded aspects.  Avalanche hazard is rated as Low on slopes less than 35 degrees.

As we should all be aware....Avalanche conditions change for better or worse continually. Backcountry travelers should be prepared to assess current conditions for themselves, plan their routes of travel accordingly, and never travel alone. Backcountry travelers can reduce their exposure to avalanche hazards by utilizing timbered trails and ridge routes and by avoiding open and exposed terrain with slope angles of 30 degrees or more. Backcountry travelers should carry the necessary avalanche
rescue equipment such as a shovel, avalanche probe or probe ski poles, a rescue beacon and a well-equipped first aid kit.

Have a safe and pleasant weekend.

Avalanche Advisory, January 25th, 2013

01/25/2013

0 Comments

 
_Good morning this is Kevin Davis with the avalanche advisory for today, January 25th, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight January 25th, 2013.  Joel Kopf has been tracking up Grandmother Mountain on the St. Joe/Clearwater Divide so we thank him for the great data from that area.

Weather 
Big sluice of weather heading your way.  Today the NWS predicts a 100% chance of 2-4 inches of new snow and temperatures rising to 36 with winds out of the south at 8mph.  Tonight the snowfall continues with 3-5 more inches, temperatures dropping to 28 and south winds at 6-8mph.  The outlook for the weekend calls for more snow with an additional 1-3 inches Saturday, Saturday night, and Sunday.  That's a potential for 18 inches over 3 days.  Temperatures will be dropping over this period of time and SW persistent at 8mph.  Avalanche danger on the rise today and decreasing slightly for the outlook period.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
Ed was out in the Twin Saddle area yesterday out of Avery and found mostly stable conditions on south and west aspects and exposed slopes, but found buried surface hoar on easterly aspects and very pronounced on sheltered slopes.  Stability tests on this layer about 10 inches to one foot deep revealed significant weakness and potential for propagating a fracture.  Data from the St. Regis Basin yesterday showed mostly stable conditions with 4-6 inches of new snow bonded well to a firm pack.  Carole did not report finding buried surface hoar in the Basin but you might be looking for it in steep, sheltered terrain. 48 hour accumulations for Lost Lake snotel were 4-6 inches so expect more on lee aspects.  With temperatures on the rise today be very careful on steep easterly aspects.  With the possibility of rain today at higher elevations be aware that the instability of the weak surface hoar layer could increase further.  Temperatures decreasing over the weekend will help in the future to create a more stable situation with less dense snow on top but watch for consistent SW winds to be developing pockets of continued weak layers on lee aspects.

The avalanche hazard rating for the St. Regis Basin and St. Joe areas are rated as MODERATE NE, E, and SE aspects steeper than 30 degrees above 5,00 feet.  The avalanche hazard is rated as LOW on steep slopes on all other aspects.

Avalanche Advisory, January 18th, 2013

01/18/2013

0 Comments

 
Good morning this is Kevin Davis with the avalanche advisory for today, January 18th, 2013.  This report does not apply to local ski areas within the forecast region and the hazard rating will remain valid through midnight January 18th, 2013.  Special thanks Silver Mountain Ski Patrol for pit data and Joel Kopf for observations and pit data from Grandmother Mountain on the St. Joe/Clearwater Divide.

Weather 
Today you have mostly sunny skies and temperatures in the low 30s and SW winds 14-18mph with higher gusts.  Tonight will be mostly cloudy with temperatures in the mid 20s and moderate winds out of the west and gusts in the 20mph range.  The outlook for Saturday and Sunday calls for more sun meaning temperatures will be rising into the 30s and the SW winds blowing at 10-14mph.  Sunday, same thing with a little less wind.  By MLK day it will get quite warm so watch for south slopes to be heating up  Avalanche danger will likely remain the same through the outlook period.

Snowpack and Avalanche Discussion 
In the St. Regis Basin yesterday, Carole and Dan found that the wind has been actively building cornices with some large cornice development observed around Steven's Peak.  With a little over 1 foot of new snow in the last week you can expect some deeper deposits on lee aspects.  Carole felt these deposits in steeper terrain would be the main concern.  They also noted buried surface hoar on sheltered aspects that sheared cleanly but with moderate force in shear tests.  To the south Ed found a more stable pack with no major weak layers revealed in stability tests on Landmark Peak.  Joel Kopf also noted mainly stable conditions on Grandmother Mountain.  Sheltered terrain, such as in the St. Regis Basin, may be allowing deeper wind deposition and may be harboring isolated pockets of buried surface hoar.  Check the upper 2-3 feet of the pack if you feel you are in terrain that has not undergone stronger, scouring winds. 

The avalanche hazard rating for the St. Regis Basin and St. Joe areas are rated as Moderate on all aspects with pockets of CONSIDERABLE on upper windloaded slopes greater than 35 degrees.
