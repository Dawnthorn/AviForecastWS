Backcountry Avalanche Forecast

East slopes WA Cascades - south of White Pass

Northwest Weather and Avalanche Center Seattle Washington
1107 AM PST Sat Feb 18 2012

This forecast applies to back country avalanche terrain below 7000 feet and does not apply to developed ski areas or highways.

Corrected zone forecast wording--

Weather Service ID

Danger Rose for Saturday
Danger Rose for Sunday
Danger Rose for Monday

Click here for complete definitions of the avalanche danger scale.

Forecast

Saturday and Saturday night: Considerable avalanche danger above 6000 feet and moderate below early Sunday. Danger increasing and becoming considerable above 4000 feet and moderate below except for high danger on north through east facing slopes above 6000 feet.

Sunday and Sunday night: Slowly decreasing danger Sunday except for a slight increase on slopes receiving sunshine. Danger continuing to slowly decrease Sunday night, becoming considerable on lee slopes above 5000 feet and moderate elsewhere. 

Monday outlook: Slightly decreasing avalanche danger expected Monday, becoming considerable on lee slopes above 6000 feet and moderate elsewhere. Slightly increasing danger expected in higher elevations Monday night.

Snowpack Analysis

Prior to the recent moderate snowfall arriving mid-late Friday and continuing Saturday morning, the old snow surface consisted mainly of one of the following:

  • generally shallow amounts of recent snow overlying strong near surface melt-freeze or sun crust layers
  • breakable surface crusts
  • thin wind slab deposits on higher elevation north through east exposures
  • shallow settled powder or recycled powder over a firm underlying crust
  • some buried but still rather reactive surface hoar layers (see below)

Regarding the buried surface hoar referred to above,...as recently as Wednesday morning avalanche control, Mission Ridge ski patrol reported "sensitive wind slabs running on surface hoar, sympathetic releases on adjacent slide paths". Meanwhile, further north along the Cascade east slopes, North Cascades Heli Ski Guides reported last Thursday that although this early February weak layer has been buried by recent small amounts of snow, it remains quite reactive in the NE Cascades and may become more so as more load is applied. This important weak layer was also recently reported near Stevens Pass, with most growth over a Feb 1 rain crust.

Increasing light to moderate amounts of snowfall at lowering freezing levels and increasing winds were deposited over this variety of pre-existing snow surfaces mid-late Friday into Saturday morning, with up to 3-6 inches of new snowfall being reported as of mid-late Saturday morning in higher elevations. As temperatures cooled during precipitation, a relatively good bond of the moderate to heavy snow accumulations formed with the most of the old snow surfaces below about 5 to 6000 feet, and this temporarily helped limit the danger increase associated with the heavy snowfall. However, a gradually weakening bond with increasing elevation above 5000 feet has combined with stronger winds to create increasingly dangerous avalanche conditions on lee slopes above 5 to 6000 feet and heightened avalanche conditions in steeper wind loaded avalanche terrain elsewhere.

Detailed Forecasts

Saturday and Saturday night

Relatively strong ridgetop and pass winds, low freezing levels and moderate snow or snow showers...heaviest near the crest...should combine to produce a further increase in the avalanche danger as slowly thickening and somewhat brittle wind slabs develop over weaker snow layers produced between breaks between showers later Friday night or early Saturday. Also, some of these gradually thickening slabs may have a poor bond with one of several crusts above about 6000 feet. As a result, increasingly dangerous avalanche conditions are expected to develop on lee slopes near higher ridges...especially northeast through southeast exposures where human triggered avalanches should become very likely and where back country travel is not recommended.

As loads increase on Saturday, some slides may start to trigger more deeply buried weaknesses, such as the early February crust/hoar frost combo mentioned in the analysis above. If so, these slides may be very sensitive and run quite fast, possibly resulting in some slides in unexpected locations or slope angles. The best advice here it to test for the existence of such a layer through snow profiles, shovel shears and compression tests...or via ski cuts on small test slopes that have low consequences if released.

At lower elevations, natural avalanches should be possible and human tiggered avalanches likely on lee, wind affected terrain, and cautious routefinding and conservative decison making are strongly urged...especially where previous surface hoar growth might be expected.

Sunday and Sunday night

Although decreasing light showers and decreasing winds are expected Sunday, cold temperatures should slow stabilization of existing wind slabs and help maintain the threat of further human triggered avalanche activity...especially on previously wind loaded terrain showing no evidence of recent avalanche activity. Also increasing sun breaks should allow for a local danger increase on more southerly facing slopes receiving sunshine during the afternoon where loose slides initiiated by snow releasing from trees or cliffy areas may trigger isolated slabs. Scattered light showers and relatively light winds late Sunday and Sunday night should allow for a slow decrease in the danger as recent wind slabs slowly settle. However, relatively cold temperatures should make such stabilization a slow process, and may begin to weaken the bond of recent snow to any of several buried crusts.

Finally, in areas experiencing clearing Sunday night, some surface hoar is likely to form, and this should be closely monitored prior to the onset of light snowfall expected to redevelop mid-late Monday...especially in the south and central.

Monday Outlook

Light snow showers should briefly develop in the north Cascades and Olympics Monday morning, with briefly increasing light snow expected in the south and central Cascades later Monday morning and afternoon. Along with continued relatively low freezing levels and light winds, mostly light snowfall accumulations should allow for a further slow decrease in the danger as recent slabs continue to strengthen and cornices settle. However, increasing ridgetop winds...slow warming...and increasing light rain or snow showers arriving late Monday night may begin to increase the danger by early Tuesday.

Backcountry travelers should be aware that elevation and geographic distinctions are approximate and that a transition zone between dangers exists. Remember there are avalanche safe areas in the mountains during all levels of avalanche danger. Contact local authorities in your area of interest for further information.

NWAC weather data and forecasts are also available by calling 206-526-6677 for Washington, 503-808-2400 for the Mt Hood area, or by visiting our Web site at www.nwac.us.

Moore/Northwest Weather and Avalanche Center Seattle Washington

The link below will take you to an experimental GIS-based map application for viewing avalanche danger forecasts for the Washington Cascades, Olympics and northern Oregon Cascades (Mt Hood area) issued by the NWAC. Please remember that the danger rose information is based on regional scale avalanche danger forecasts, and should not be used for slope-specific danger determinations.

Use care and common sense when viewing and applying snow stability information on this GIS display. These forecasts are intended to provide both history and overall trends in snow pack stability. Remember that the snow pack is highly variable, very dynamic, and snow structure and related stability can and do change within a few feet and often within a few hours, sometimes dramatically.

Remember, YOU are responsible for using and applying any information found here. Choice not Chance causes most avalanche accidents.
